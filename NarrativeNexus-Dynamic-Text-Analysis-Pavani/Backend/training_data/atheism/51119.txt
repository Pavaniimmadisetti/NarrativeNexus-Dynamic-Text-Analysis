Gospel Dating and Biblical Scholarship

John's Gospel and Synoptic Relationship

John has a quite different theology than the synoptic gospels. There is evidence that John knew Luke and the content of Q was known to him, but not in a canonized form. Q-oid quotes appear in John but not in the codified way they appear in Matthew or Luke.

These quotes are considered similar enough to point to knowledge of Q itself rather than an entirely different source. This Q-hypothesis is central to understanding the relationship between the four gospels and their historical development.

Dating and Manuscript Evidence

Earlier manuscripts of John have been discovered providing evidence for dating. Dating from around 200 CE, these manuscripts shed light on the chronological order of the gospels. The question of whether John was written after the other three gospels remains subject to scholarly debate.

Understanding the chronological relationship requires examining manuscript evidence and textual patterns. The dating of ancient texts relies on paleography, historical references, and cross-textual analysis between the gospel accounts.

Source Analysis

The usual explanation for the time order of Mark, Matthew and Luke considers textual dependencies. Matthew appears to have read Mark, and Luke read Matthew and probably Mark as well. John's relationship to these texts follows a similar pattern of literary dependence.

Assuming John knew the content of Luke's text, the evidence is not overwhelming. Scholars debate whether literary parallels indicate direct knowledge or independent development of similar material. These discussions involve careful analysis of vocabulary, theology, and narrative structure.

Gospel Authorship

That John was a disciple is not generally accepted. The style and language together with the theology are usually cited as counterarguments to traditional attribution. The argument that John was a disciple relies primarily on the claim in the gospel of John itself.

Is there other evidence for apostolic authorship? Historical scholars generally accept that the gospels were composed at least one step and one generation removed from the events they describe. In modern times, one step removed is considered problematic for historical accuracy.

Textual Editing and Transmission

Mark has clearly been edited throughout its transmission history. The most obvious editing involves the ending of Mark, which presents a problem of incompleteness. The oldest versions give a strong sense of incompleteness, with the shortest versions breaking off in midsentence.

The most obvious solution is that part of the text was lost at some point. Material from verse 9 onward is clearly later and represents a synopsis of the end of Luke. This suggests scribal additions and editorial intervention in the transmission process.

Reconstructing Original Texts

One does not know what the original text of Mark looked like. Arguments based on the current Mark are therefore weak when attempting to establish the relationships between gospels or to make claims about the historical Jesus. Textual criticism must account for the layers of editing and transmission.

Scholars must carefully distinguish between what can be known about the original texts versus what we can know about the texts as preserved in our manuscripts. This distinction is crucial for historical-critical analysis of the gospel accounts.
